\section{Conclusion}

What began as an experiment with GraphRAG applied to six immunology articles evolved into a domain-specific agentic pipeline validated across six drug discovery domains, 111 documents, and 25 user acceptance tests. The trajectory of that evolution---from similarity-based graph assembly to comprehension-based extraction---is itself the central finding of this work.

Similarity-based approaches to biomedical knowledge graph construction, including GraphRAG and related embedding-retrieval methods, produce graphs that reflect lexical co-occurrence patterns in source text. These graphs are useful for information retrieval but do not capture the mechanistic, causal, and regulatory relationships that define biomedical knowledge. Epistract demonstrates that parallel LLM extraction agents, constrained by a domain schema and validated by molecular structure tools, can produce knowledge graphs grounded in scientific meaning rather than statistical proximity. Across six therapeutic domains---neurogenetics, oncology, rare disease, immuno-oncology, cardiovascular inflammation, and GLP-1 competitive intelligence---the system achieved 100\% entity type coverage and 93\% relation type coverage with zero retraining or domain-specific fine-tuning, because the extraction agents comprehend the source text rather than measuring distances within it. The sixth scenario additionally validated multi-source corpus assembly from PubMed, Google Scholar, and Google Patents, demonstrating that the pipeline can extract molecular identifiers from patent documents and produce graphs structured for competitive intelligence analysis.

The contribution is twofold. The method---agentic extraction with typed domain schemas, parallel agent dispatch, and molecular validation via RDKit and Biopython---establishes a reusable pattern for building knowledge graphs from scientific literature. The tool---an open-source, MIT-licensed Claude Code plugin installable in one command---makes that method immediately accessible to bioinformatics teams in pharma and biotech. The system works today, on real corpora, with committed and reproducible results.

The human-AI collaboration model through which epistract was built is a third, less conventional contribution. Production-quality scientific software---including the extraction pipeline, six validated test scenarios, project documentation, and this paper---was constructed through intensive interactive sessions where human scientific judgment directed AI implementation capabilities. This is not a claim about AI autonomy; it is a claim about the productivity of a specific collaboration pattern that other computational biology teams can adopt.

Several directions remain open for future work. Export to Neo4j or similar graph databases would enable production-scale queries and integration with existing knowledge management infrastructure. Combining the extracted knowledge graph with vector embeddings could support hybrid retrieval that leverages both structural relationships and semantic similarity. External enrichment from PubChem, ChEMBL, and UniProt APIs would ground extracted entities in canonical identifiers and cross-link to established biomedical databases. Finally, extending the domain schema to additional therapeutic areas---infectious disease, neuroscience, metabolic disorders---would test the generalizability of the extraction approach and expand the system's utility across the drug discovery pipeline.
